This may be the most uncomfortable finding of the year: although 88 percent of organizations worldwide now use AI, only one in four has moved 40 percent or more of its AI experiments into production. Adoption is nearly everywhere — real, scaled value only at a few. Anyone who wants AI as a competitive advantage must clear exactly this hurdle. It is the most important one of all, and it determines whether enthusiasm becomes business value or only a growing stack of proof-of-concepts.

## The Finding

The gap between "we are doing something with AI" and "AI creates measurable value" is wider than the general euphoria suggests. According to Deloitte, 25 percent of companies currently report having moved at least 40 percent of their pilots into production — three quarters fall short. At least 54 percent expect to reach that level within the next three to six months.

The widely cited MIT NANDA study "The GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025" draws the line even more sharply: based on interviews with executives, an employee survey, and analysis of roughly 300 public AI rollouts, it concludes that around 95 percent of generative AI pilots deliver no measurable effect on the profit-and-loss statement and only about 5 percent create significant value. Important for context: this figure is contested. Critics point out that the study defines success very narrowly — as P&L impact within six months — and thereby excludes efficiency gains, quality leaps, and longer-term effects. The exact percentage should therefore be read with caution. The direction, however, aligns with all major surveys: many pilots, little production.

## Why It Stalls

The core of the problem is a misunderstanding of what production requires — as opposed to a pilot. Both feel like the same project, but they are not.

A pilot runs with a small team, over a few months, with clean data in a sandboxed environment. Production, by contrast, demands infrastructure investment, integration into existing systems, security reviews, compliance checks, monitoring, and ongoing maintenance. The MIT study names the root cause the "learning gap": generic tools shine for individuals but fail in the enterprise because they do not learn from — and adapt to — the organization's own workflows. Their finding on "build vs. buy" is also notable: solutions delivered by external vendors reach productive use roughly twice as often as in-house builds — not because buying is inherently better, but because specialized partners optimize for workflow integration and adoption. Complementing this, the IDC/Lenovo "AI CIO Playbook 2025" (cited in several industry analyses) offers a sobering ratio: for every roughly 33 proof-of-concepts started, only four go live.

The environment into which you scale is more complex than ever. McKinsey shows that the number of monitored infrastructure endpoints, microservices per core platform, and productive releases per year each rose many times over between 2015 and 2025. In such distributed, multicloud environments, failures appear as cascading patterns — a pilot that works in isolation runs straight into this at go-live. The Stanford AI Index adds the "jagged frontier," the uneven capability boundary: a top model wins math competitions yet reads analog clocks correctly only in roughly half of cases; AI agents solve around two thirds of real computer tasks but fail on about every third attempt. This unevenness is precisely why a pilot can shine while production still fails. An AI lead in healthcare describes the consequence in the Deloitte report as "pilot fatigue": many experiments, no scaling plan, ultimately failed value creation.

## What Leaders Do Differently

Successful companies do not treat pilots as isolated experiments but as stepping stones to production. They clarify from the outset the realities that will decide later — system integration, data rights, operational ownership — and design for live operation from the start. The MIT study offers a clear success pattern: the few winners choose a tightly scoped, high-value use case, integrate it deeply into a real workflow, and, where it makes sense, bring in specialized partners instead of building everything in-house. Leaders are also honest about the jagged frontier: they deliberately seek use cases where AI is reliably strong and build human checkpoints for unreliable areas.

## What This Means for Scaleups and Mid-Market

Organizations with limited resources especially tend to create breadth instead of depth: many small experiments because they are quick and cheap. That is exactly the trap. The good news: the bottleneck is organizational, not technological. For a scaleup, the deliberate decision to bring two pilots truly into production instead of ten can be the difference between measurable advantage and expensive busywork. The build vs. buy finding is particularly relevant here: those with little internal platform experience often move faster and more safely with a specialized partner than with an ambitious in-house build.

## Action Items for Practice

1. **Make scalability a hard selection criterion.** Allow only pilots with a realistic path to production; everything else is deliberately not started.
2. **Define measurable business value from day one.** Without a clear production success criterion, there is no driving force to finish the journey from experiment to live operation.
3. **Choose the use case narrowly and deeply.** One clearly defined problem, deeply integrated into a real workflow, beats the broad, shallow "AI everywhere" experiment.
4. **Weigh build vs. buy honestly.** For each initiative, assess whether a specialized partner with a learning, integrated solution reaches the goal faster than an in-house build.
5. **Test against the jagged frontier.** Deliberately stress-test a model on edge cases, poor data, and boundary situations before it goes live.
6. **Plan realistic time and cost buffers.** Assume production rollout takes a multiple of pilot duration, and communicate that openly.
7. **Assign clear operational ownership.** Define who monitors, maintains, and evolves the system after go-live — otherwise it stalls between project and IT.
8. **Measure success by production use.** One metric in the steering committee: what percentage of our experiments run in production and create value?

## Conclusion

Most companies have learned to experiment with AI. What still stalls is the step after that: turning a working trial into a reliable system embedded in everyday work. This step is more expensive, slower, and more uncomfortable than a pilot — but it is the only one that creates real value. Research disagrees on the exact success rate but not on the direction: many pilots, little production. Those who plan for live operation from the start, integrate one narrow and valuable use case deeply, and honestly assess whether a partner gets there faster will go further than someone running a hundred pilots in parallel. The rule of thumb stays simple: better to bring two things properly into production than to merely try ten.

More than three in four companies now factor the country of origin of an AI solution into vendor decisions. At the same time, "AI sovereignty" has become a central organizing principle of national policy according to Stanford — from the EU through Japan to dozens of emerging economies. For German and European mid-market companies, this is no geopolitical footnote. It is changing who they build with, where their data resides, and which vendors even make the shortlist.

## The Finding

Sovereign AI has reached the boardroom — as a strategic question, not a compliance topic. According to Deloitte, 83 percent of companies consider the topic at least moderately important, 43 percent very or extremely important; two thirds (66%) are concerned about their dependence on foreign AI technology. 77 percent actively factor country of origin into vendor selection, and 58 percent build their stack primarily with local providers.

A McKinsey survey of roughly 300 executives, investors, and government representatives from December 2025 underscores the urgency: 71 percent classify sovereign AI as an "existential concern" or "strategic necessity" for their goals. The Stanford AI Index 2026 shows why the topic is escalating now: the first prohibitions under the EU AI Act took effect, the United States moved toward deregulation, Japan, South Korea, and Italy passed national AI laws, and more than half of newly adopted national strategies come from developing countries. Notable for European companies: globally, the EU enjoys more trust than the United States or China in regulating AI effectively.

## Why It Stalls

Sovereign AI means that a country — and the companies operating there — develop and operate AI under their own laws, on controlled infrastructure, and with locally governed data. But what does "sovereignty" mean in practice? A precise research framework helps. The paper "AI Compute Sovereignty" by Hawkins, Lehdonvirta, and Wu (Oxford Internet Institute, 2026) breaks the question into three layers: first, how much AI compute capacity physically sits in the country; second, the nationality of data center owners; and third, the nationality of accelerator chip suppliers. Empirically, across nine major cloud providers (roughly 70 percent of the market), national control is rare on all three layers — location in-country does not automatically mean control.

How concentrated this dependence is becomes stark in the Stanford report: the United States hosts 5,427 data centers — more than ten times any other country; Germany follows with 529, the United Kingdom with 523, China with 449. The hardware layer is even more concentrated: practically every leading AI chip is fabricated by a single foundry in Taiwan (TSMC). The Brookings analysis "Is AI Sovereignty Possible?" (February 2026) adds the regulatory dimension: the EU (risk-based framework), the United States (sectoral, voluntary approaches), and China (algorithm and content regulation) are diverging, increasing compliance complexity for cross-border companies — and stressing that strategic planning must consider dependencies across all layers. The regional differences are uncomfortable for Europe: according to Deloitte, only 11 percent of companies in the Americas source their stack predominantly from abroad, versus 32 percent in EMEA.

## What Leaders Do Differently

In practice, a former observability lead at a major telecommunications group reports that international customers increasingly insist on in-country infrastructure — including compact, "distilled" language models built directly in each country to satisfy import and export rules.

Leading companies address this along the three sovereignty layers with focus. They assess which data and workloads must remain in-country, who owns the infrastructure in use, and where the chips come from. McKinsey emphasizes that compute strategy becomes the core question: cloud, on-premise, hybrid, or edge — the choice of processing locations must simultaneously cover data residency, regulation, and performance. Leaders build infrastructure to serve multiple regulatory regimes at once, making sovereignty a built-in property of their offering rather than a reaction to individual customer demands.

## What This Means for Scaleups and Mid-Market

At its core, sovereign AI is not about owning technology but about strategic independence. Those who address sovereignty requirements proactively gain more than compliance: less regulatory uncertainty, greater customer trust, and a strong position in industries where sovereignty dominates — public sector, healthcare, financial services, critical infrastructure, defense. For an export-oriented mid-market company, demonstrable data control may decide market access. And high trust in EU regulation is a potential sales argument abroad for European providers.

## Action Items for Practice

1. **Think sovereignty in three layers.** Clarify for your critical systems: where compute sits, who owns it, and where the chips come from.
2. **Map data and workloads.** Define which data and processing steps must not cross national or regional boundaries.
3. **Make supply-chain dependencies visible.** Know where you rely on a single vendor — and plan alternatives.
4. **Choose compute strategy deliberately.** Decide per workload between cloud, on-premise, hybrid, and edge, aligned to multiple regulatory spaces.
5. **Prioritize model transparency.** Where traceability matters, prefer models with disclosed properties or open weights.
6. **Plan regulation early.** Incorporate the EU AI Act and relevant national laws into product planning before they become blockers.
7. **Communicate sovereignty actively.** Make demonstrable data control a sales argument, not only an internal checkbox.

## Conclusion

For a long time, only one question mattered in AI: what can the technology do? A second is now equally decisive: where is it built, and who controls it? Research shows that sovereignty is more than server location — it encompasses in-country compute capacity, infrastructure ownership, and chip origin, and genuine national control is rare on all three layers. For European companies, that means more effort, because solutions must adapt to different markets. It is also a real opportunity: those who clarify early which data may sit where, and who can demonstrate control over their AI, will become the preferred partner in many industries. Sovereignty is no longer a burden for legal alone but a piece of competitiveness.

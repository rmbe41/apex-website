Access to AI tools grew 50 percent within a year — from under 40 to roughly 60 percent of employees. That sounds like success. Yet of those with access, fewer than 60 percent use AI in daily workflows, and this pattern has barely changed year over year. Anyone who measures AI success by distributed licenses is optimizing the wrong number.

## The Finding

The numbers tell a two-part story. On one side, diffusion: according to Deloitte, 11 percent of leading companies grant access to more than 80 percent of their workforce. The Stanford AI Index places this historically — generative AI reached roughly 53 percent of the population within three years, faster than the PC or the internet, and four in five students use it. On the other side stands stagnation in actual use at work: fewer than half of those with access use AI in everyday practice.

That the benefit is real is established by peer-reviewed research. Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond show in "Generative AI at Work" (Quarterly Journal of Economics, 2025) that an AI assistant in customer support raises productivity on average by roughly 14 percent — with the largest gains among less experienced employees, who reach the level of veterans faster. The Stanford AI Index cites productivity gains of 14 to 26 percent in areas such as customer support and software development. Effects are unevenly distributed: for tasks requiring stronger judgment, they are weaker or even negative. The value is there — it only arrives where use succeeds.

## Why It Stalls

A look at sentiment explains much. According to Deloitte, among non-technical employees only 13 percent are highly motivated, 55 percent at least open, 21 percent use AI only when they must, and 4 percent actively avoid it. The Stanford AI Index adds a notable detail: when asked how AI affects their own work, 73 percent of professionals expect a positive effect — but only 23 percent of the general public. A gap of 50 points. Skepticism in the workforce is not an outlier but part of a broad pattern.

Paradoxically, the MIT NANDA study finds that while official pilot projects stall, a "shadow AI economy" flourishes — a large share of employees use private AI tools such as ChatGPT daily, although only a fraction of companies provide official licenses. People want to use AI — often ahead of the formal rollout. A sensitive dynamic documented by the Stanford-adjacent research team around Brynjolfsson: in highly AI-exposed occupations, employment among young entrants (ages 22–25) fell relatively by roughly 13 percent; among young software developers, roughly 20 percent since late 2022. The distinction between automation and augmentation matters: where AI replaces people, employment falls; where it augments them, less so. Those who fear making themselves redundant use AI more hesitantly — activation is therefore also a question of trust and clear communication about the future of roles.

## What Leaders Do Differently

High-impact rollouts do not start top-down but with empowered employees who experiment, share early wins, and become internal advocates. Directives from above create obligation, not habit. The effective mechanism combines grassroots adoption with visible leadership support. It is also smart not to fight the shadow AI economy but to legitimize it: where employees already use private tools, companies should offer secure, approved alternatives with real workflow relevance. And because productivity gains are largest among less experienced employees, targeted investment there pays off.

## What This Means for Scaleups and Mid-Market

Change management resources are often scarce — and that is exactly why the convenient path is chosen: buy the tool, enable access, move on. The result is expensive licenses with low utilization. The key insight: the bottleneck is not the tool but adoption. Smaller organizations even have an advantage — short lines, visible leadership, and quick success stories are easier to create than in a corporation.

## Action Items for Practice

1. **Measure use, not access.** Track how many employees actually use AI in daily work — the only metric that correlates with value.
2. **Make the shadow AI economy visible and legitimize it.** Offer secure, approved tools instead of ignoring or banning private use.
3. **Start with less experienced employees.** Productivity gains are largest there — a fast, visible win.
4. **Turn the enthusiastic 13 percent into champions.** Give them platform and time to share their concrete successes.
5. **Design training by role.** Replace generic training with real use cases from each person's daily work.
6. **Let leadership lead visibly.** When executives use AI themselves and talk about it, that works stronger than any directive.
7. **Address fear of displacement openly.** Communicate clearly how roles will change — security about one's own future increases willingness to use AI.
8. **Focus on augmenting use cases.** Start where AI makes people better, not where it replaces them.

## Conclusion

Many companies believe they have rolled out AI because they distributed licenses. But an enabled tool that nobody uses creates no value — it only costs money. Real success begins when people voluntarily adopt the technology into daily work, and that does not happen on its own. Research shows two things clearly: the benefit is real and largest among less experienced employees, and many employees already use AI — often ahead of the official rollout. At the same time, concern about one's own role is justified and widespread. Those who invest in role models, practical training, and open communication, and channel existing use into safe paths, extract multiples from the same licenses. The right question is therefore not "How many have access?" but "How many does AI actually help at work today?"
